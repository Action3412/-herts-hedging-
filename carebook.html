Watering

Get this one thing right

20L × 2/wk
Per plant, for the whole first growing season

Deep, infrequent watering drives roots downward. Daily light watering keeps roots at the surface where they'll fail. Tap a card to see what each pattern looks like.

Under-watering?

Tap to see ›
SignsWilting outer leaves first. Yellowing on the oldest, lower leaves. Brown, crispy edges. Soil bone dry and powdery 5cm down.

Over-watering?

Tap to see ›
SignsYellowing across the whole plant. Rapid leaf drop. Soil sodden, sour-smelling. Leaf spot disease. Looks like drought, but isn't.

Just right?

Tap to see ›
SignsFirm soil that crumbles in the hand. Soft, glossy new growth. Leaves reach full size by mid-summer. New shoots extending each week in season.
Pruning

Tap a hedge. See when to cut it.

Each species has its own schedule. The wrong month can cost a whole season of growth.

Beech

Fagus sylvaticaTap for pruning ›
When to cut

One annual trim in August. That's it. Holds dead leaves through winter for privacy.

Hornbeam

Carpinus betulusTap for pruning ›
When to cut

Same as beech. August, once a year. Tougher than beech in heavy clay or wet ground.

Yew

Taxus baccataTap for pruning ›
When to cut

Two cuts: late May and late August. Will regrow from old wood, unusually for a conifer.

Box

Buxus sempervirensTap for pruning ›
When to cut

Two cuts: late May and late August. Use clean, sharp shears. Box blight is real.

Laurel

Prunus laurocerasusTap for pruning ›
When to cut

Use secateurs, not shears. Shears tear leaves and they brown at the edges. Trim August.

Photinia

Photinia × fraseriTap for pruning ›
When to cut

Light trim after the red spring flush. Avoid hard cuts. A second light tidy in late summer if needed.

Mulch

The one job to never skip

50mm of well-rotted compost or bark, around the base of every hedge, every March. The single most valuable annual task in a garden.

Suppresses weedsThey steal water and nutrients. Mulch shuts them out.
Locks in moistureReduces evaporation by ~30%, dramatically reducing how often you need to water.
Feeds the soilSlow-release nutrients all year. Worms and soil life do the rest.
Feeding & soil

Feed the soil, not the plant

Well-mulched, biologically active soil makes most fertiliser unnecessary. Where feeding is genuinely needed, slow and steady wins.

✓ Do

Mulch in March

50mm of well-rotted compost or bark. For most hedges, most years, this is all the feeding required.

✓ Do

Slow-release organic for hungry hedges

Privet and laurel can take a spring feed of pelleted chicken manure or blood, fish and bone.

✗ Don't

Use lawn feed near hedges

Within a metre of hedge roots, lawn nitrogen encourages weak leggy growth. Keep the two apart.

✗ Don't

Apply weed-and-feed

The systemic herbicide can be taken up by hedge roots. Real harm done before any symptoms show.

A £15 soil pH test from the garden centre will tell you more than three years of guesswork.
Plant health

Catch problems early

Walk the hedge once a month. Most problems are easy to fix in their first weeks, expensive once they've settled in.

Yellow lower leaves

A common worryTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Over-watering or poor drainage. Counter-intuitively the leaves look thirsty. Cut back watering, check drainage.

Bare patches in box

In an established box hedgeTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Box blight. Isolate the patch. Sterilise tools between cuts. Treat or replace. Act fast — it spreads.

Brown tips in spring

After a hard winterTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Winter desiccation. Drying winds plus cold roots. New growth will mask it. No action needed.

Sticky leaves, ants climbing

A summer surpriseTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Aphid honeydew. Usually self-corrects as ladybirds catch up. Hose off if persistent. Spray only as a last resort.

Sudden dieback in summer

A serious signTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Honey fungus, vine weevil or Phytophthora. Get someone to look. Send us a photo before you spend money.

White powder on leaves

Late summer onwardsTap for cause ›
Likely cause

Powdery mildew. Improve airflow by lifting low branches. Affected leaves drop in autumn. Not usually serious.

No. 01

Yew can live for 2,000+ years

Tap ›
The factThe Fortingall Yew in Perthshire is estimated at 2,000–5,000 years old. Many old churchyard yews are older than the churches they stand next to.
No. 02

Beech holds its leaves through winter

Tap ›
The factIt's called marcescence. Young beech and the lower branches of mature trees hold dead brown leaves through winter — handy for privacy when other hedges go bare.
No. 03

Mulch cuts watering by ~30%

Tap ›
The factA 50mm mulch layer reduces evaporation from the soil dramatically. In a hot summer it can be the difference between thriving and barely surviving.
No. 04

Hedges sequester carbon for life

Tap ›
The factAn average mature hedge stores about 80kg of carbon per metre over its lifetime, plus shelters wildlife, breaks wind and reduces noise pollution.
No. 05

Box has been clipped for 2,000 years

Tap ›
The factThe Romans were the first to clip box into formal shapes. The word 'topiary' comes from Topiarius, the Roman gardener responsible for it.
No. 06

Hedges are wildlife corridors

Tap ›
The factA single 100m hedge can support over 40 species of birds, mammals and invertebrates — linking otherwise isolated patches of habitat.

Closing

When to do it yourself, and when to call us

You can probably do it yourself

  • Mulching, hand-weeding, watering
  • Light trim of a young, easy-to-reach hedge
  • Spotting and reporting any pest or disease signs
  • Small-scale planting (5 to 10 bare-root plants)
  • Lawn mowing and basic feeding

Worth bringing us in

  • Anything over 2m tall — height + ladders multiply risk
  • Sculpted, formal or topiary cuts
  • Renovation of an old or overgrown hedge
  • Suspected disease — box blight, honey fungus
  • Planting more than 20m, or any specimen over 1.8m
