Planting & supply
When is the best time to plant a hedge?+

Container-grown specimens can be planted year-round, but autumn (October to early December) and early spring (March to April) are ideal: soil is workable, rainfall does most of the watering for you, and roots establish before the demands of summer growth. Root-balled stock has a tighter window of late autumn to early spring.

The Care Guide year-round calendar shows exactly what to plant when, month by month.

How quickly will a new hedge look established?+

That's the advantage of working with mature stock: we typically plant specimens that are already 1.8m to 2.5m tall, so the visual impact is immediate. Full knitting-together of the canopy then happens over the first 12 to 24 months as plants settle and put on new growth.

Which species do you most often recommend?+

It depends on site, aspect, soil and the look you're after. Portuguese Laurel (Prunus lusitanica) is our most-requested for its glossy evergreen leaf and reliable habit. English Yew makes a magnificent formal hedge for traditional gardens. Photinia 'Red Robin' is excellent for a touch of colour. We'll always walk the site with you before recommending.

Do you guarantee the plants you supply?+

Yes. When we both supply and plant, we guarantee establishment subject to our aftercare schedule being followed. We provide written guidance on watering, mulching and seasonal care, and we offer optional maintenance plans for clients who'd rather we handle it.

What if I just want plants supplied, not planted?+

Supply-only is fine. Many landscape architects, garden designers and DIY-confident clients buy from us and arrange their own planting. We give you the same advice on positioning, depth, spacing and aftercare, and provide written guidance to take home. Bare-root, root-ball and Airpot-grown stock are all available.

Seasonal care & maintenance
When should I trim my hedge?+

It depends on species. Beech, hornbeam and mixed native hedges get one annual trim in August. Yew, box and privet take two cuts a year, late spring and late summer. Laurel benefits from secateurs rather than shears, to avoid the ugly cut-leaf look. Anything over 2 metres is worth getting help for.

The Care Guide calendar shows the right month for every job, by category and by month.

How much should I water my new hedging?+

Newly planted hedging needs 20 to 30 litres per plant, twice a week, throughout the whole first growing season. Deep infrequent watering drives roots downward; shallow daily watering keeps them at the surface where they'll struggle in the next dry spell. Established hedging rarely needs supplementary water at all.

More on this in the watering essentials and our full irrigation guide.

Do you cover lawns and the wider garden, not just hedges?+

Yes. Our Care Guide covers lawns (the simple four-job year), pruning by species, mulching, feeding, plant health and seasonal planning. Browse the full year-round calendar and use the category filter to focus on what you need. For ongoing care across a full garden, our maintenance plans can include any combination.

Irrigation & drainage
Do I really need an irrigation system?+

If you have new planting, hedges over a metre long, or you go away in summer: almost certainly. A reliable irrigation set-up takes the strain out of watering and is the single biggest peace-of-mind investment most garden owners can make. It also usually pays back through plants that survive rather than need replacing.

Read the full irrigation guide for the detail.

What kind of irrigation do you recommend?+

It depends on what you're watering and how much you want to think about it. Drip irrigation is best for hedges and borders. Sprinklers suit lawns and broad open areas. Soaker hoses are a simple retrofit for existing beds. Tap timers and smart controllers automate any of the above. We plan, install and maintain at any scale and on any budget.

The full breakdown is here, including what we'd suggest for different setups.

My garden floods or stays soggy. Is that a drainage problem?+

Almost always, yes. Yellowing lower leaves on hedges, lawns that puddle after rain, beds that won't dry out in spring — all classic drainage signs. We assess drainage as part of any garden project and design French drains, soakaways or soil amendments where they're needed. No point watering soil that water can't leave.

More on drainage in the Care Guide.

Essentials

The fundamentals of good care

Five principles that underpin every healthy garden, whatever the size or species. Get these right and most of the rest takes care of itself.

Watering & irrigation

Water early, water deep

The single most common mistake in garden care is shallow, frequent watering. New planting needs deep, infrequent soaks that drive roots downward in search of moisture. Established plants rarely need any supplementary water at all.

  • Best time of day: early morning. Avoid evening (encourages fungal disease) and midday (high evaporation losses).
  • Newly planted hedging: 20-30 litres per plant, twice a week, for the whole first growing season.
  • Established hedging: only during prolonged drought, more than three weeks without meaningful rain.
  • Lawns: 25mm of water once a week is far better than 5mm every day.
Read the full irrigation guide
Pruning

The right cut at the right time

Pruning timing depends on species and intent. The wrong month can cost a whole year of growth, or worse, set a hedge back permanently. Formative, maintenance and renovation pruning each follow different rules.

  • Beech & Hornbeam: a single annual trim in August. Will hold dead leaves through winter for privacy.
  • Yew & Box: two cuts per year — late spring and late summer.
  • Laurel: use secateurs, not shears, to avoid ugly cut leaves that brown at the edges.
  • Flowering shrubs: prune immediately after flowering, not before.
  • Anything taller than 2m: consider getting help. Most accidents happen on ladders.
Feeding & soil

Feed the soil, not the plant

A well-mulched, biologically active soil makes most fertiliser unnecessary. Where feeding is genuinely needed, slow-release organic feeds in spring outperform fast-acting chemical ones almost every time.

  • Annual mulch: 50mm of well-rotted compost or bark, every March. The single most valuable garden task.
  • Spring feed for hungry hedges (privet, laurel): blood, fish & bone, or pelleted chicken manure.
  • Avoid lawn feed near hedges: within 1m of hedge roots, lawn nitrogen encourages weak leggy growth.
  • Test before you treat: a £15 pH test from a garden centre will tell you more than three years of guesswork.
Plant health

Catch problems early

Most hedge and shrub problems are easy to fix if spotted in the first few weeks. Box blight, honey fungus, drought stress and pest damage all show telltale signs long before they become serious. Walk your boundaries once a month.

  • Yellow lower leaves: usually overwatering or poor drainage, not under-feeding.
  • Bare patches in box: probable blight. Act fast — isolate and treat or replace.
  • Brown tips in spring: winter desiccation. New growth will mask it; no action needed.
  • Sudden dieback in summer: get someone to look. Honey fungus, vine weevil and Phytophthora all look similar at first.
Lawn care

The four-job lawn year

You can have a lovely lawn with four well-timed jobs per year. Doing more rarely helps. Doing them in the wrong order definitely hurts.

  • March: first cut on a high setting. Spring feed.
  • May: scarify or rake out moss. Top-dress thin areas with a sand-soil mix.
  • August: if dry, leave the grass longer. Let it go dormant rather than scalping.
  • October: autumn feed. Aerate compacted areas with a garden fork.
A closer look

Irrigation, considered

Take the strain out of watering. Done properly, an irrigation set-up is invisible, automatic, and the difference between a garden that survives summer and one that flourishes.

A reliable irrigation system is the single biggest peace-of-mind investment a garden owner can make. No more dragging hoses across the lawn at dusk. No more wondering whether the new hedge made it through your week away. No more guesswork about how much is enough. It costs less than most people think to do well, and a lot less than replacing dead plants does.

From a single tap timer feeding a couple of pots, to a fully zoned drip system across an estate boundary, we plan, install and maintain at any scale and on any budget. The right setup matches the planting, the soil, the water source and how much you want to think about it. We talk all of that through before any work begins.

The main types

Pick the right method for the job

Sub-surface / drip

Drip irrigation

Slow, targeted, surgical. A pressure-regulated pipe runs alongside the planting and emits water directly into the root zone. Uses 30 to 50 per cent less water than overhead methods, and almost nothing is lost to evaporation. The best long-term answer for hedges.

Best for: hedges, borders, raised beds, new planting
Overhead

Sprinklers

The classic. Pop-up or fixed sprinklers spray a wide arc — perfect for lawns and broad open areas where coverage matters more than precision. Much better distribution than a hose, far less effort. Pair with a rain sensor and a smart controller for serious water savings.

Best for: lawns, open areas, broad planted zones
Surface

Soaker hoses

The simplest retrofit. Porous rubber hose that "weeps" water along its full length. Snake it through an existing bed, connect to a tap timer, walk away. Inexpensive, no electricity needed, no major install. A genuinely sensible entry-level option for smaller gardens.

Best for: existing beds, smaller gardens, modest budgets
Automation

Tap timers & smart controllers

The brain. A battery tap timer turns any of the above unattended for weeks at a time. Smart controllers with rain sensors and live weather feeds skip a cycle when rain is forecast. We work with Hunter, Rain Bird and Hozelock kit depending on what the job calls for.

Best for: any of the above — turns manual into automatic
Often forgotten

Drainage matters as much as watering

Plants drown as easily as they dry out. A garden that puddles after rain, a lawn that stays soggy into spring, or a hedge with yellowing lower leaves — these are usually drainage problems, not watering ones. We assess drainage as part of every irrigation plan, and design French drains, soakaways or simple soil amendments where they're needed. No point watering soil that water can't leave.

When to ask

DIY, or get the professionals in?

A rough guide. We'd always rather give you honest advice than sell you work you don't need. If in doubt, send us a photo.

You can probably do it yourself

  • Light formative trim of a young, easy-to-reach hedge
  • Mulching, hand-weeding, watering
  • Small-scale planting (5 to 10 bare-root plants)
  • Lawn mowing, basic feeding, scarifying with a rake
  • Walking the boundary to spot and report any pest or disease signs

Worth bringing us in

  • Anything over 2m tall — height and ladders multiply risk fast
  • Sculpted, formal or topiary cuts
  • Renovation of an old, overgrown or one-sided hedge
  • Planting more than 20 metres, or container-grown specimens over 1.8m
  • Diagnosing disease — box blight, honey fungus, Phytophthora
  • Removal, especially where root systems or barriers are involved
